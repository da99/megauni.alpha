NIH Funding

From: http://www.dilbert.com/blog/entry/problems/?Page=2


shagbark on Jul 9, 2010 said:
> What problem does your company's product try to solve?

We don't know. The NIH pays us to produce data, that scientists are supposed to use to cure diseases. But we don't know much about who they are, or what they do with the data.

> What is the biggest problem for people in your profession?

I have no way of telling whether I'm improving the process, because there's no way to know whether my results are right or wrong.

> What is the biggest problem in your industry?

1. The NIH likes to fund research and the development of new data sources, but isn't so keen to fund making the research results available, or keeping old data sources up-to-date and available. So we put a lot of effort into developing data sources, and then when they're ready, we lose funding and take them offline. The NIH's current solution is to have us promise in the contract to keep the data up-to-date and online forever, on our dollar. This is impossible.

2. Computer scientists are the people who understand math, and large complex computational systems, and how to develop systems that take a question and compute an answer for it. But biologists are in charge; and they think the computer scientists are just geeking out when they try to explain that it's possible to infer things, not just to make giant databases of things biologists have found.

> What is the biggest problem/constraint in your current project?

About a hundred people around the world are competing to develop the same product, in little groups of 1 to 5 people, with little cooperation between groups, all funded by government research grants.
